Product teardown

Spotify — the conversation layer it never built

Spotify is the best playback and personalization engine in music — and one of the weakest at conversation. Its social surface is an afterthought: a desktop-only Friend Activity sidebar, Blend, and the once-a-year Wrapped spike. So the most social part of music has leaked out of the app into group chats and Instagram Stories — tools where a track isn't playable and the moment disappears.

If I owned this, I wouldn't bolt a public feed onto the main app — it would fail, because most of Spotify's base treats it as a utility and would never post, so the feed looks empty and dies. I'd start narrower: a lightweight "share to a small circle" action on the now-playing screen, defaulting to private groups rather than a public broadcast. Private, low-stakes audiences are where people actually share music; public feeds invite performance anxiety and silence.

The trade-off is reach for density — fewer posts, but from people who'll actually engage. And the metric I'd hold it to isn't shares; it's weekly returning sharers, because that's the loop that proves the behavior is real, not a launch novelty.

How I prioritize

I run RICE to size incoming requests, then level the score against the longer-term roadmap — a high-RICE quick win can still be the wrong thing if it pulls the team off a strategic bet. When the two conflict, I make the call explicitly and reconvene with stakeholders on the why and the timeline. Example: a welcome-flow request scored low on RICE but mattered for fraud prevention, so it jumped the queue — a deliberate, communicated override, not a silent reshuffle.

Catching scope before it catches you

On the transfer comms rebrand, the brief said "27 letters." Doing the math — 27 letters × 1,300 customers × 50 states — the real surface was ~175,000 variations and a six-month build. Instead of absorbing that, I found the leverage: the cover letter was shared across nearly every customer, so shipping the CTA on page one got the redirect live in a month and protected ~$500K in overhead. In regulated systems, the leverage is in the architecture, not the feature list.
